The Godfather and Succession:
A Study of Power, Psychology, and Inheritance

At first glance, The Godfather and Succession belong to entirely different worlds. One tells the story of a 20th-century mafia dynasty. The other dissects a 21st-century media empire. One is steeped in old-world crime and loyalty; the other in boardrooms, shareholder meetings, and corporate warfare.

Yet beneath their surface differences lies the same central question:

What happens when power must pass from father to child?

Examining both works through psychological, financial, and filmmaking perspectives reveals how deeply connected they truly are.

I. The Psychological Lens: Fathers, Sons, and the Burden of Approval

At the heart of both narratives stands a dominant patriarch:

Vito Corleone
Logan Roy

They are not merely fathers; they are institutions. Their identities are inseparable from their empires. In psychological terms, they function as archetypal authority figures — powerful, intimidating, and emotionally withholding.

The Psychology of Succession

Both stories revolve around the psychological tension of inheritance:

Michael Corleone initially resists power.
Kendall Roy desperately seeks it.

Michael’s arc is about transformation. He distances himself from the family business, but circumstances pull him back in. Over time, he suppresses emotion, internalizes his father’s ruthlessness, and ultimately becomes a colder version of Vito.

Kendall’s arc, by contrast, is marked by instability. He seeks validation rather than authority. His ambition is fueled less by strategic vision and more by a desire for paternal approval. From a psychological standpoint, Kendall struggles with individuation — he cannot fully separate his identity from his father’s shadow.

Love as Power

In both universes, affection is conditional. Approval is earned through strength. Weakness is punished.

The result is emotional repression, sibling rivalry, and loyalty driven by fear. These are not merely family dramas — they are studies of how power structures distort intimate relationships.

II. The Financial Perspective: Corporate Empire vs Criminal Empire

From a financial consultant’s viewpoint, The Godfather and Succession are case studies in succession failure.

Lack of Institutional Transition

Both Vito and Logan fail to establish a clear, stable succession framework.

Instead of:

Structured leadership transition
Governance planning
Objective merit-based evaluation

They rely on:

Emotional testing
Personal loyalty
Power maneuvers

This creates organizational fragility.

In The Godfather, instability leads to violent consolidation of power. Michael eliminates rivals and centralizes authority to stabilize the family.

In Succession, instability manifests as boardroom coups, hostile takeovers, shareholder pressure, and market vulnerability.

The difference lies in method — bullets versus ballots — but the structural flaw is identical: leadership is personalized rather than institutionalized.

Legacy vs Growth

The Corleone empire focuses on protection and territorial control. Its economy is built on loyalty networks and influence.

Waystar Royco operates within global capitalism. Its value depends on acquisitions, media influence, and shareholder confidence.

Yet both systems hinge on a single risk factor: the competence of the heir.

Can the successor preserve and expand the empire without destroying it from within?

III. The Filmmaking Perspective: Power Through Visual Language

Cinematically, both works reinforce their themes through deliberate visual and narrative techniques.

Lighting and Tone

The Godfather uses warm, shadow-heavy cinematography. Darkness dominates interiors, symbolizing secrecy, hierarchy, and tradition. Power feels intimate and old-world.

Succession adopts cooler tones — glass buildings, sterile offices, muted greys. Power feels corporate, detached, and modern.

Yet in both, isolation is emphasized. Characters often appear alone within expansive spaces, visually reinforcing the loneliness of authority.

Framing the Successor

Michael’s evolution is visually clear. He moves from the margins of family gatherings to the central seat of power. By the film’s conclusion, the door literally closes on his wife as he fully assumes his father’s role.

Kendall, on the other hand, is frequently framed as small within vast corporate environments. Despite wealth and status, he appears diminished — visually communicating his internal instability.

Dialogue as Weapon

The Godfather employs restrained, deliberate dialogue. Meaning lies in subtext and silence.

Succession uses rapid, cutting exchanges. Language becomes corporate weaponry — sarcasm and strategic ambiguity replace overt threats.

Different eras. Same brutality.

Conclusion: Feudal Power and Corporate Capitalism

Ultimately, The Godfather represents a feudal model of power — loyalty, bloodline, territory.

Succession reflects late-stage capitalism — shareholder value, media dominance, brand control.

But both are tragedies of inheritance.

Michael becomes his father — at the cost of his humanity.

Kendall attempts to become his father — and fails to escape his shadow.

In both stories, succession is not merely a transfer of assets. It is a transfer of psychological burden, moral compromise, and identity.

And in both, the greatest question is not who inherits power — but what that power ultimately destroys.

— Debolina Tapadar
